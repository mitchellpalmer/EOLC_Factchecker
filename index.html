The Facts about End of Life Choice

Have you been duped?

Here are the 10 lies being spread about the End of Life Choice referendum from people who want to sow fear, uncertainty and doubt to suit their own ideology. You may have already seen this blatant misinformation, so we're here to bust their myths and help you make an informed decision based on fact. Click on each question to see the real answer to their leading questions.

The most important thing to remember is that this referendum is about giving choice to terminally ill, mentally competent, adult New Zealanders who have run out of all other options.

Can the End of Life Choice Act be changed after the referendum?
The End of Life Choice Act has already been passed by Parliament and will come into force if a majority of New Zealanders vote for it at the referendum. It was debated in Parliament for nearly two years, one of the longest processes in the history of Parliament. Parliament put numerous safeguards into the bill, which overseas experience shows are effective in keeping individuals safe from coercion.
Can those with a diagnosed mental illness use assisted dying?
The Act says you ARE NOT eligible to choose assisted dying because of a mental illness, you MUST have a terminal illness. Anyone eligible to seek assisted dying must be evaluated by two doctors and, if either doctor has doubts, a psychiatrist must show the person is mentally competent to choose assisted dying.
Do the doctors who sign off on the assisted dying need to have met the person before?
The doctor must be intimately familiar with the patient's circumstances. The Act requires they communicate with the patient 'at regular intervals determined by the progress of the person’s terminal illness'. They are required to explain and understand the patients prognosis, options and intentions. They must consult with other professionals involved in the patient’s care and seek to talk with their family.
What's the shortest time the assisted dying process could take?
The Ministry of Health advises that the shortest period possible from an initial application to the assisted death is around three weeks. Given the number of safeguards and consultations involved, this is probably a fast estimate. International experience shows arbitrary delay periods do not make the law safer, but some people die badly waiting for the period to end.
Does a doctor have to talk to the person’s family before providing assisted dying?

The law requires doctors to encourage patients to talk to others, including their family and friends. The only times when this is likely to not happen is when the patient has no family to speak to or there are good reasons not to involve them. In Oregon, where a similar law has been in place for more than 20 years, less than 5% of patients have chosen not to inform their families.

Do independent witnesses need to make sure that the person’s choice is free from any pressure?

Yes. The law specifically requires an independent medical practitioner selected by the Ministry of Health to examine the patient. They must determine that the person themselves believes assisted dying is the right choice. If this doctor or the patient's own doctor are unsure about whether the patient is capable of making a free and informed decision, they must consult a psychiatrist.

Would this Act make it legal to turn off life support?

It is already legal to turn off life support, however, this is not relevant to this referendum. Very few people on life support would be competent to request assisted dying under this Act. This Act is about providing choice and relieving suffering at the end of life for those who can exercise it.

Does an eligible person need to be in any physical pain to request assisted dying?
Yes. To be eligible for assisted dying, a person must be:
  1. terminally ill, with less than 6 months to live; and
  2. in an advanced state of irreversible decline in physical capability; and
  3. experiencing unbearable suffering that cannot be relieved in a manner considered tolerable.
Do people have to try any treatment options first before requesting assisted dying?

The Act is a last resort. It can only be used by people who 'experience unbearable suffering that cannot be relieved in a manner that the person considers tolerable' and who 'understand their other options for end-of-life care'.

Can an eligible 18-year-old receive a lethal dose without their parents knowing?

In Oregon and Canada, the average age of a person accessing assisted dying is 75. There are very few 18 year olds who would be eligible to use this Act and, unless there were strong reasons do so, it is very unlikely that two doctors would be comfortable approving assisted dying for a young person who had not consulted their family.
